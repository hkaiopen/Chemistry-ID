--- Explanation for De_ionic scan ---
De_ionic is the depth of the ionic Morse well. A very shallow well (low De_ionic)
cannot hold the wavepacket long enough to reach the crossing; the molecule
dissociates directly on the ionic surface without crossing -> low yield.
A very deep well (high De_ionic) traps the wavepacket, requiring higher initial
velocity to escape and reach the crossing; if the velocity is fixed, the yield
decreases again. The optimum occurs at De_ionic ≈ 3.0 eV where the wavepacket
has enough energy to cross but not too much to overshoot.

--- Explanation for initial_mean_v scan ---
The initial mean velocity determines the kinetic energy when approaching the crossing.
Low velocity leads to slow passage through the crossing, which gives a low Landau‑Zener
probability (adiabatic behavior, stays on ionic surface) -> low dissociation yield.
High velocity increases the non‑adiabatic transition probability, but if too high,
the wavepacket may cross too quickly and also the force may change; yield increases
monotonically in the scanned range, so we extrapolate that very high velocities
(e.g., 5.0 Å/ps) give the best yield (~65%).

--- Explanation for coupling scan ---
The electronic coupling V12 directly enters the Landau‑Zener exponent.
Lower V12 gives lower transition probability? Wait: The exponent is negative, so
smaller V12 -> less negative exponent -> larger P (more likely to switch).
Thus decreasing coupling increases the dissociation yield, as seen in the scan.
However, too low coupling (V12 → 0) makes the transition purely diabatic, which
may also affect the energy distribution. In our range, lower = higher yield.
